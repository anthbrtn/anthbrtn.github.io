Anthony Burton / Platform Cultures / Amsterdam

Writing about platform cultures, digital subjectivity, and the styles of contemporary reaction.

Anthony Burton is a researcher of internet culture whose work sits between social theory, media studies, and digital methods. He is currently a postdoctoral researcher in the Media Studies department at the University of Amsterdam, after completing a Ph.D. in Communication at Simon Fraser University.

Across scholarship, collaborative projects, and teaching, he studies how platforms give shape to subjectivity, truth claims, political feeling, and the minor vernaculars through which networked publics come to understand themselves.

Current Concerns

His recent work turns around authenticity as both a cultural demand and a technical effect. Rather than treating authenticity as a purely moral or interpersonal category, Burton approaches it as something produced through interfaces, institutions, temporalities, and rituals of verification. That line of inquiry is central to Algorithmic Authenticity, and continues in ongoing work on appification, digital libertarianism, and technofascism in Silicon Valley.

Selected Terrain

Earlier and parallel publications have examined misinformation moving across platforms, Canada's far-right media ecology, the rationalism of incel subcultures, the memetic life of figures such as Wojak, and folk theories of persecution surrounding deplatforming. The thread running through these projects is an interest in how media systems organize appearance, cognition, and antagonism at once.

Institutional Work

Burton has held roles and fellowships across the Digital Democracies Institute, the Infoscape Research Lab, and the Open Intelligence Lab, and co-founded the Digital Citizenship Cultures Initiative. He has also taught and led research seminars on data studies, qualitative methods, and symbolic infrastructures of distrust.

Formation

His path into research runs through literature, philosophy, journalism, and editorial work. That mix matters to the tone of the site as much as to the scholarship itself: analytic, but not overly sanitized; readable, but suspicious of false clarity; formal without becoming flat.
