Anthony Burton / Media Studies / Amsterdam

Research on authenticity, reaction, and the forms of life built by media systems.

Anthony Burton is a postdoctoral researcher and lecturer in the Media Studies department at the University of Amsterdam. He completed his Ph.D. in Communication at Simon Fraser University in 2025, where he was a Mellon-SFU Data Fluencies Fellow at the Digital Democracies Institute.

His work moves between social theory, cultural analysis, and digital methods, with a sustained interest in how internet platforms organize truth claims, styles of selfhood, and political antagonism.

Research

Much of Burton's work begins from the intuition that the contemporary internet is not just an information environment but a scene in which subjects learn how to appear, desire, imitate, and authenticate themselves. This has led him to study appification, misinformation, reactionary media, memetic vernaculars, and the infrastructure of platform time.

Across these topics, he is interested less in isolated bad content than in the social and technical forms that make certain ways of speaking, posting, and belonging feel persuasive, native, or inevitable.

Writing

Recent publications include the open-access book Algorithmic Authenticity and essays on cross-platform misinformation, Canada's far-right media, incel rationality, memetic figures such as Wojak, and deplatforming. Forthcoming and in-progress work extends these concerns into technofascism, digital libertarianism, and the layered temporalities of apps.

Taken together, the writing traces how platforms reshape public reason not only through moderation or recommendation, but through the ordinary aesthetics of participation.

Projects and Teaching

Alongside individual scholarship, Burton has co-led Beyond Verification at the Digital Democracies Institute, co-founded the Digital Citizenship Cultures Initiative, and worked with both the Infoscape Research Lab and the Open Intelligence Lab. He teaches across data studies, research methods, and digital culture; recent courses include Critical and Applied Data Studies at Simon Fraser University and The Memeplex: Tracking Symbolic Infrastructures of Distrust at the University of Amsterdam.

Background

Before and alongside academic work, Burton moved through journalism and cultural production, including roles at the Literary Review of Canada, Investment Executive, and Canadian University Press. That background still shapes the cadence of the work: careful about language, attentive to genre, and interested in how institutions narrate their own authority.

He studied English Literature and Philosophy at the University of Toronto, works in English and French, and builds research software when useful, including web archiving and scraping tools.
